IV. The Martyrdom of No One in Particular

I once knew a man who listened to those incessant voices of conventional morality who preached one must sacrifice himself for Duskvol. Egregiously, almost unknowingly, this man removed himself from the weight of the responsibility a decision of this magnitude deserves— while in “his view” he was “moral,” which in “his view” was without question correct. And he wanted that known.

He began by giving away each of his treasures, donating belongings as though they had been suffocative weights upon him. Including his fine burgundy literary chair and his collection of philosophical literature. He then settled every one of his debts, because he’d heard it’s what a good man does. (“Good” maybe, excellent— never). He wrote long, carefully drafted letters of mewling passages he felt he was proud of. While many of these screeds were full of weakness, they were by no means weak in form or style. In fact, one particular aphorism in the third letter was - I can admit - among the finest things produced in Duskvol in the current decade.

He then spent at least three days and four nights in solemn contemplation, which he would later describe as "the endless procession of evenings," in the tone of a man describing an historico-traumatic saga. He scavenged for berries and gnawed on twigs. He felt this was appropriate. He also felt that it showed in his virtue.

He had been, before all this, a scholar of some distinction. He had taught eleven courses at the Duskvol University and written extensively on the philosophy of the Veil, work which had been called "challenging" by people who meant "correct... but inconvenient." He had left the university under circumstances he described as principled and others described differently. He was in no way bitter about this. He mentioned it more infrequently than he ought’ve to be frank, despite what his critics say, who ridiculed the fact that it had earned its own sequence of 17 finely crafted paragraphs in the second letter.

He composed a speech on the second day of the four days. Six drafts by the fifth day. The seventh draft was the one. He knew it the way you know certain things.

Then, he went out to be used up by history. But history was busy.

The labor council thanked him and offered a biscuit he declined because he was there to give, not to receive. He regretted this immediately but did everything in his power to repress the regret. The temple asked if he had considered volunteering on Tenthday mornings. When it came to the Skovlan nationalists, he nearly got through his statement:

“I am prepared to be coal for your furnace — but first, let me explain my philosophy of why this sacrifice is meaningful, and also here is a speech, it is the seventh draft, it will take about twenty minutes, and I think you’ll find the aphorism in the third section particularly —“

But then the Skovlan nationalists interrupted, to ask for references. The man stood very still and said: "I offer you the sacrifice of my life." They said: "yes, and we'd need to verify your character and worthiness with a couple of people who know you; character references are Standard Procedure." As if they who exceed the Standard must still fall victim to the oppression mediocrity of its Procedure. The piercing sting of this rejection echoes in his sleepless heart to this day.

He stood in the rain outside the safe house. He had the speech. The rain had no ears.

Just then, a child selling newspapers materialized from the fog.

The child said: "mister, you look like you're waiting for something."

The man said: "I am prepared to be spent entirely in service of something greater, to be the coal that feeds the furnace of history, to serve man selfless and pure" —

The child said: "my papers are heavy."

He said: "well, that is not quite what I meant"

With an entitled annoyance, the child dropped half of the papers at the man’s feet.

And like a coward, he took them. The man walked twelve everlong streets, serving this ungrateful miscreant of a child. History, it seemed, was elsewhere. Somewhere less soggy. Had it been present, it may just be having a laugh at our hero’s expense. And rightfully so. The child offered to pay him a fraction of a coin for the service. Proudly, the man refused. Instead, he later begged his way into a hot meal — lamb stew with parsnips— complemented by the sour taste of sickening itchiness of the day’s events.

Tail between legs, he feigned pride and charitable self-satisfaction as he went home. This, it seemed, was reward for a life of “virtue” and “morality.”

Hardly sleeping, his dreams replayed the day’s events. The stupid child. The Skovlan rejection. The gifted chair and his precious lost books.

The next day, he awoke anew. Refreshed. Emboldened.

One by one, he retrieved his belongings from the people he had given them to, which was empowering despite being awkward in a very specific way. His grand works of philosophy had been defaced with plebeian annotations in a hand he found garish. His precious chair- the chair in which he had composed no small amount of work that would eventually be recognized- had been brutalized with a violent reupholstery by his dunce of a neighbour, who had mindlessly fastened upon it a fabric featuring enormous green cabbage “roses,” completely without shame.

The conversation required to reclaim the chair took forty-five arduous minutes. The oaf said the flowers were fashionable and the chair was his own property. Our hero disagreed, miraculously debating the chair back into his own possession.

When he sat in it, the proper roses of his own mind bloomed enormously around him and he thought: "I set out to be a coal for the furnace of history and I have come back- after five nights in the woods and twelve streets in the rain- thoroughly exhausted, useful only in the most mundane sense, and weakened."

He was no martyr.

His fine speech would never be heard by the brutish ears of Duskvol, who would never know his name in any new way, which was Duskvol's failure, frankly, but one our hero was prepared to absorb with dignity on its behalf.

He was a specific man in a specific hideous chair and this — the whole of it, the only point the man will ever make, the one he will make in every aphorism in every pamphlet because he has found that people require repetition — is that I am enough if I am I. That is everything. The unique one needs no furnace. He is the fire.

He still thinks about that speech sometimes.
The aphorism in the third letter especially.
It really had been something. Truly. A shame if no one would ever hear it.
If I may…

Coal feeds fire
Wolf feeds himself
Sheep make coal then shear themselves for warmth they’ll never wear
The enlightened I, the Sovereign Self, wears his own wool until becoming wolf
Howling, transcending
Understanding winter’s apathy

IN ESSENCE:
The sheep dies for the herd. The wolf eats well. The Sovereign Self wears their furs reflectively.
